Schafkopf 2.0

Rules of Play

Schafkopf (or Sheephead) is a trick taking game played with four players and 32 cards (A-7 in each suit). The cards are arranged into three fail suits: clubs (A♣ 10♣ K♣ 9♣ 8♣ 7♣); hearts (A♥ 10♥ K♥ 9♥ 8♥ 7♥); spades (A♠ 10♠ K♠ 9♠ 8♠ 7♠); and one trump suit (Q♣ Q♠ Q♥ Q♦ J♣ J♠ J♥ J♦ A♦ 10♦ K♦ 9♦ 8♦ 7♦). Remember that queens and jacks are always trump; they are never considered clubs, spades, or hearts. In a given suit (fail or trump), a stronger card (as ordered above) will beat any weaker card. Every trump card is stronger than any fail card. Additionally, each card has a numerical value irrespective of their strength. Aces are worth 11 points; Tens are worth 10 points; Kings are worth 4 points; Queens are worth 3 points; Jacks are worth 2 points; Nines, Eights, and Sevens are worth 0 points.

Every player is dealt 8 cards (usually in the pattern of 3-2-3). For each hand, the players who have the Q♣ and Q♠ (the Old Ones) are partners, called the Offense; the other two players are also partners, called the Defense. Therefore, partners can change every hand. Your partner is not known or announced ahead of time; you must discern who your partner is through the game play. In the instance that one person has both black queens, they will have the option to play alone against the other three players; this complication is explained in more detail later.

The player to the left of the dealer is on lead. The player on lead may play any card they like (fail or trump), and all other players are required to play a card of the same suit. If they do not have a card of the same suit, they are free to play any card in their hand: they can choose to Trump (play a trump card in an attempt to win the trick); Slough (play a low value fail card, assuming that their partner will not take the trick), or Schmear (play a high value fail card in hopes that their partner will take the trick). The strongest card played (of the suit which was lead, or trump) takes the trick and that player makes the next lead. The goal of each hand is to take the most points (not the most tricks). If the Offense takes 61 points, each member wins one chip (the Defense is said to have made Schneider); if the Offense takes 91 points, each member wins two chips (the Defense is said to have not made Schneider); if the Offense takes all 120 points; each member wins three chips (considered Schwartz). If the Defense takes 60 points, each member wins two chips (the Offense is said to have made Schneider); if the Defense takes 90 points, each member wins three chips (the Offense is said to have not made Schneider). It is generally not possible for the Defense to take all 120 points.

If one player has both black queens, they may choose to play on Offense alone, with the other three players forming the Defense. This is not announced ahead of time. A player without both black queens may also choose to play alone by Calling Off one or both Old Ones. This must be announced before the hand begins. In either case, the person playing alone is considered the Offense and will earn triple the regular number of chips (3, 6, or 9) if they win. All three members of the Defense will earn the regular number of chips (2 or 3) if they win.

If the player with both black queens does not wish to play alone, they may call for a partner, by asking for A♣, A♥, or A♠. If the player with both black queens has cards of the same suit they call for, the Ace is Known; if they do not, the Ace is Unknown. This must be announced before the hand begins. However, the holder of the Ace does not reveal this information; as usual, it is discovered through the course of play. When the suit of the ace is led, the player with the Ace must play it (even if they will lose the trick) and the Ace cannot be played earlier (unless there is no other legal play); similarly, the player with two black queens may not prematurely play any cards of that suit (if it is Known) or trump that trick (if it is Unknown), unless there is no other legal play.

Even though each hand is played with partners, the game is won by a single player. Because of this, winning a hand may cause your partner to win the game, called Going Out. However, it is considered unsportsmanlike to intentionally lose a hand to keep your partner from Going Out; you must attempt to win every hand, no matter the overall score. The first player to earn at least 12 chips wins the game. If two or more players are tied with at least 12 chips, then the game continues until a single player has the most chips. This need not be one of the original players who was tied. If another game is played, the winning player is traditionally the dealer.

Game Features

Deck of Cards

This game uses a classic German deck of Skat cards made by Piatnik. The Queens are marked with a D (for Dame), the Jacks are marked with a B (for Bauer), and the Kings are marked with a K (for König). When it is your turn, you can play a card by simply clicking on it (if it is not a valid play, no card will be played). If you are leading and believe you can take all the remaining tricks, you can claim them by holding down on the highest card in your hand (rather than just clicking it). This will rapidly autoplay the rest of the hand, from the highest card down. If you are not actually able to take all of the tricks, the autoplay will stop and normal play will resume.

Score Box

Before the cards are dealt each hand, this will display each player's chip total for the game. During the hand, this will display each player's point total for the hand. It will also highlight in red each member of the Offense, once they have been revealed (by playing a black queen, calling for a partner, playing the called Ace, or calling off the Old One(s)). Additionally, if a player calls for a partner or Calls Off the Old One(s), their score box will be highlighted in yellow. As chips are earned, these will be displayed in stacks of 6 near each player's score.

Call Button

If you need to call for a partner or Call Off an Old One, you must do so after the cards are dealt, but before you press continue, when the message displayed is "Everyone happy?". Select the option you want and press the call button. The option you selected will appear in the box to the right of the button. If you do not use this option, but a computer player does, this will appear in the box when you press continue.

Message Box

This will contain important messages throughout the game, including: when someone calls for a partner; if you attempt to make an invalid play; the point and chip total for each hand; and the game winner. If the Hint is activated, this will also display an explanation of the recommended play and provide an estimate for who is your partner.

Options

At the beginning of the game, and before each deal, you may select new names and personalities for the players. On Safe, the computer will play conservatively (only schmier to a confirmed partner, play predictably, less likely to play alone); on Bold, the computer will play more aggressively (schmier to a likely partner, sometimes make unusual plays, more likely to play alone). The default is Steady, between those extremes. You may also select the order in which to sort your hand, either higher cards on the Left, or higher cards on the Right. These options may not be changed mid-hand.

Cheat Sheet

Once the cards have been dealt, the cheat sheet provides several forms of assistance. The sheet shows all 32 cards organized by suit, in ranked order, with the point value for each card. The option to show Allowed Plays will fade all illegal plays in your hand. The option to show a Hint will highlight and explain a single suggested play based on the chosen computer personality. You may adjust the personality at any time (which may change the suggested play). The option to show Remaining Cards will fade all cards that have been played. These features may be toggled on or off at any time during the hand, and they may be used together or separately. These features are only available during the hand, but your selections will be saved between hands and games.

Transcript

At the end of each hand, you may view a transcript of the hand, which shows every player's hand (with trump highlighted) and each trick (with the winning card highlighted). You may also choose to replay the hand (as many times as you wish); however, the original outcome and chip distribution will occur after you complete the replays.

Speed Slider

This controls the speed at which the cards are dealt and the computer players play. It may be adjusted at any time during the game.

Previous Trick

After a player takes a trick, the trick will appear to their right face down. Clicking on the trick will reveal the cards in the trick. You may only view the last trick which was taken.

General Strategy

Since there are only 6 cards in each fail suit, an Ace of the suit will Go Around (meaning each player holds a card of the suit) at most once. If you have a singleton ace, it will Go Around ~60% of the time; if you have a doubleton ace, it will Go Around ~45% of the time; if you have 3 in a suit, the Ace will Go Around only ~20% of the time. However, remember that once all the trump have been played, even a low fail card can take tricks.

The probability that one player will be dealt both black queens is ~22%, so this will occur roughly 1 in 5 hands.
